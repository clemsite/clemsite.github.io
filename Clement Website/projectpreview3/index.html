Walking Leo

Kareem, Rayan and I emerge onto the street out of the ominous black gates of their house on Grove End Road, and head east towards the tube stop. Leo is golden, glowing with perfect precision, and pulls Rayan along. We are talking, or maybe rather I am talking, non-stop, and I am holding a skateboard. How many times have we walked this path before?

There is a cricket game at Lord’s Cricket Ground and everyone is blobbed in our way as we walk past the station, wearing those orange-and-yellow-striped ties that everyone wears when there’s a game at Lord’s. We all agree we don’t know why everyone wears them when there’s a game at Lord’s. We turn south off Acacia Road onto St. Anne’s Terrace, which becomes the High Street. The Duke of York bursts with people who either want to watch the game or already saw it. Rayan and Leo are briefly lost in the crowd, possibly stuck, and I deeply empathize with Rayan’s stuckness, but am too delighted to emerge from the crowd myself to try to help him. He emerges several seconds later anyways, Leo tugging him out by the lead.

We turn onto the cobbled Charles Lane, just south of Gelato Mio, still talking. Several small children once tried to mug me, Shahid and Tina here. At the end of it, we turn right, and going south on Charlbert Street there is a miraculous conflux at its intersection with Allitsen Road, wherein Mo and Adib park right next to where we are walking and yell something that makes us jump before we realize it’s them. Two women in hijabs walk by, and they grin at something Mo says to them in Arabic as he gets out of the car. I don’t know Mo but he has a very thin nose and is wearing a fedora. Adib is just so happy to see Leo, and after all it’s a beautiful and gorgeous day that occasionally vibrates with satisfaction. Leo is grinning, and he’s happy to see Adib as well. We walk towards the New Inn, where Adib and Mo want to get a pint or something. Kareem’s hair is insane. I think I give Adib my American phone number because we’re going to meet them later.

We keep going down Charlbert and finally get to the park, and I skateboard down the little hill, and then the bridge across the canal, and up to the Outer Circle road. The small greebles of the pavement vibrate up through the wheels and massage my feet. There’s something electric about concrete with moss growing out of it that makes itself known to me. I wait for the twins, and we cross the road together. Rayan lets Leo off the lead, and he surges forward, sprinting, and then jumps over the fence and then jumps back over and heads for the thick of the park. Rayan and Kareem walk on either side of me as I roll on the skateboard. Leo stops way ahead of us on the path to look back at Rayan, posing in the dappled tree light and smiling. He waits for a few seconds and then hurls himself forwards again towards the grass and the people. There are so many people. Ohh no Rayan says, because Leo is, like, in someone’s picnic. Everyone is playing softball, including the woman guarding the picnic. She doesn’t seem to be too bothered by Leo even though I strongly feel that he ate something, but she doesn’t have a real face right now so it’s hard to tell.

Now we are walking again, Leo back on the lead because it must be softball day in the park and there are all these people from America acting like Americans. Seven glistening shirtless men with tiny shorts come charging at us on the path, and I know they are going to trample us or stick us with spears. They are in a pack, I don’t know, primordial warriors or something. But they are just training. I want to tell them it’s irresponsible to run in a pack like that because it’s terrifying. We stop and watch a softball game for a while. Kareem’s hair is like small-knotted rams’ horns, and he has dark rings under his eyes. Softball, I have just realized, is very very interesting.

The team fielding the ball right now is a single organic machine that stops the ball midflight, absorbs its momentum with an elasticity that flings the ball back to home at the same speed it left. We stare at them for a long time, entranced, discussing softball. The men in tiny shorts seem to be doing laps, and keep running past us as we head to where the Honest Sausage used to be. With each pass, they are still causing me to feel the wide-eyed terror of a sheep about to be slaughtered, but I’m getting used to it. I get back on the skateboard and a breeze kicks up at my back. The grass is so rich with color it ripples. I kick forward again and I can feel my t-shirt billowing deliberately. I take the left fork of the path, ride along the pavement between fields of tall yellow-tipped grass, soaring, the tear-dropped flaxen tips so fine that they never reach a point.

Now Mo and Adib have found us. Adib is really angry because he tossed back a stray softball that rolled onto the path, and no one said thank you. We carry on, following the curved path that will bring us alongside the zoo. The trees begin arching over our heads. Like the grass, and Leo’s beautiful golden hair, their leaves seem too precise to find completion. Flashes of sharpness glow where the sun sifts through the upper canopy. How’s Saudi, Mo? Kareem asks. Mo doesn’t like it, he says. We all agree we love St. John’s Wood. Some animals in the zoo make noises. In the shifting, layered procession of large walking groups, Kareem falls back towards Adib, I am talking to Mo. He works for a defense contractor in Saudi, and he explains what that means. I nod, smiling. It is completely incomprehensible to me.

Adib, Mo and Leo go off with Adib’s girlfriend to Primrose Hill, but we head back to Kareem and Rayan’s place where we have forgotten that Julian is waiting for us.
